Memory Technique

Spaced Repetition

Review information at scientifically-optimized intervals. The single most effective technique for long-term retention.

How It Works

Spaced repetition exploits the spacing effect: we remember information better when reviews are spread out over time rather than crammed in one session. Each time you successfully recall an item, the interval until the next review increases. Your brain strengthens the neural pathway each time it successfully retrieves the memory.

This tool uses a modified Leitner system with intervals of: 1 minute, 10 minutes, 1 hour, 4 hours, 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks, 1 month. Items automatically advance through the levels as you recall them.

    The Science

    Spaced repetition was pioneered by Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885, who discovered the forgetting curve: we lose about 50% of new information within an hour, and 90% within a week — unless we review it. Each review flattens the forgetting curve, making the memory more durable.

    Modern research confirms that active recall (retrieving information from memory) is far more effective than passive review (re-reading). This is called the testing effect. Combining active recall with spaced intervals produces some of the largest effect sizes in all of cognitive psychology (d > 1.0).

    For neurological recovery, spaced repetition is especially valuable because it works with impaired encoding. Even if initial encoding is weak, repeated retrieval attempts strengthen the trace over time. It builds confidence by showing tangible progress.
